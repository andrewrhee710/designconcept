Good Example: The MET

How does this website utilize a good grid-based layout?

The MET Website

Notice how this website utilizes a hierarchy grid system. The important information (buying tickets) is displayed front and center by itself, and as a user scrolls down the exhibits are organized in a neat grid fashion that allows viewers to easily read and discover new exhibits.

Grids do not need to be made of clear black lines either. The MET builds their grid with subtle differences in shades of color for text boxes, blank space between sections, and photos to mark out different divisions between exhibits.

Scanning through the website, the effects of good grid-based design are really apparent. It makes easy for a user to navigate through exhibits, ticketing buying procedures, and important updates even if they have not been to an art museum before.

The header of the page is the largest, allowing the MET to showcase a video displaying different aspects of the museum. This draws users in, and the convenient button in the center takes users straight to information about purchasing tickets.

The grid based layout can be seen as the user scrolls down. The most important information is put near the top: where the MET is located at. The two museum locations are organized clearly and professionally, allowing users to see the hours and dates the two MET locations are open.

The exhibits are found below the locations, organized in a more slim, horizontal style. No bold black lines are used to divide the exhibits from one another. Instead, they use photos and slightly darker shades of background to seperate the sections from one another.
